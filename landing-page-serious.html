Wikifreedia

Knowledge without consensus

Every Wikipedia article converges to a single version. But who decides what stays and what gets deleted? A handful of anonymous administrators operating behind usernames, enforcing a "neutral point of view" that is anything but neutral.

Larry Sanger, Wikipedia's co-founder, now warns that the platform he helped create may be "entirely controlled by the CIA." Conservative sources are blacklisted. Dissenting views are systematically removed. The promise of a democratic encyclopedia has become a carefully managed narrative.

Wikifreedia takes a different approach. Built on Nostr, every article exists as a cryptographically signed event. There is no canonical version. No central authority. No deletion.

Instead of forcing consensus, we embrace plurality. Every perspective can coexist. Your social graph becomes your editorial filter. The people you trust shape what you see.

Two principles

01
Reality exists, but our grasp of it is always partial.

We each see the world through our own lens—shaped by culture, experience, and belief. Instead of pretending we can achieve perfect objectivity, we should acknowledge these limitations and learn from contrasting perspectives.

02
In the realm of knowledge, consensus is unnecessary.

Physical communities need shared rules to function. But knowledge thrives on disagreement. Forcing a single narrative destroys nuance and eliminates the very debates that advance understanding.

Wikipedia's problems

  • Anonymous control

    High-level editors hide behind usernames. Their real identities, affiliations, and motivations remain unknown. Yet they decide what millions read as "truth."

  • Systematic bias

    Entire categories of sources are blacklisted. Alternative media, conservative outlets, and independent journalists are deemed "unreliable" by anonymous administrators.

  • Forced neutrality

    Complex topics are reduced to a single approved narrative. The "Neutral Point of View" policy becomes a tool for enforcing conformity rather than presenting multiple perspectives.

  • Deletion as censorship

    Inconvenient information doesn't just get edited—it gets erased. Once deleted, there's no way for readers to even know what was removed or why.

How Wikifreedia works

Every article is a Nostr event—cryptographically signed and distributed across relays. No server to shut down. No database to corrupt. No admin panel to capture.

When you disagree with an article, you don't edit it—you fork it. Your version exists alongside the original. Readers see the version created by people they trust, or they can explore all perspectives.

This isn't chaos. It's how knowledge actually works. Scientists publish competing theories. Historians offer different interpretations. Philosophers debate fundamental questions. Wikifreedia simply makes these natural disagreements visible rather than hiding them behind forced consensus.

Wikifreedia is alpha software. It breaks. It evolves. It needs contributors who understand that the path forward isn't through better gatekeeping but through radical transparency.
