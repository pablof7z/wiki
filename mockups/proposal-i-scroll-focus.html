Capitalism

Capitalism is a natural extension of humanity's innate propensity to truck, barter, and exchange one thing for another. It is not the product of legislation or design, but of a tendency as old as civilization itself.

When individuals pursue their own self-interest, an invisible hand guides markets toward outcomes that benefit society as a whole.

The division of labor, enabled by free exchange, multiplies productive capacity beyond what any central authority could achieve. A pin factory with ten specialized workers produces forty-eight thousand pins a day; a single worker, fewer than twenty.

The role of the state is limited but necessary: enforce contracts, protect property rights, provide public goods no private actor would supply. Beyond this, government intervention distorts the natural price signals that coordinate millions of independent decisions into a coherent economic order.

Capitalism

Capitalism is a historically specific mode of production defined by private ownership of the means of production and the exploitation of wage labor. It is not "natural" — it emerged through specific historical processes of dispossession and enclosure.

The capitalist extracts surplus value from workers, paying them less than the value they produce. This is not a flaw in the system — it is the system.

The apparent freedom of the market masks a fundamental asymmetry. The worker is "free" to sell their labor — or starve. Competition drives capitalists to intensify exploitation and replace human labor with machinery, creating a reserve army of the unemployed that depresses wages further.

The state under capitalism is not a neutral arbiter but an instrument of class domination, enforcing the property relations that sustain bourgeois power. Capital accumulation inevitably concentrates wealth and deepens the contradictions that will lead to capitalism's own dissolution.

Capitalism · Version 3 of 3
Capitalism

Capitalism, in its pure form, is a system of voluntary exchange between free individuals, rooted in the absolute right to self-ownership and private property. What most people call "capitalism" today is actually corporatism — a corrupt partnership between big business and big government.

The state is not capitalism's guardian — it is its greatest enemy. A truly free market requires no state at all.

Every government intervention — taxation, regulation, licensing, central banking — is a coercive violation of individual rights that distorts market outcomes and creates the very problems it claims to solve. Minimum wage causes unemployment. Regulation creates monopolies. Central banking creates boom-bust cycles.

Private arbitration, insurance, and defense agencies can provide every service government monopolizes, more efficiently and without coercion. The market does not need a referee — it needs to be left alone.
