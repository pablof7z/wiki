Encyclopedia Entry
Capitalism

Capitalism is an economic system characterized by private ownership of the means of production, in which investment decisions are made by private actors and prices are determined by competition in markets.

The system generates prosperity through competition and innovation, though its critics note it produces inequality and periodic crises. The relationship between capitalism and the state remains one of the most contested questions in political economy.

The apparent freedom of market exchange is celebrated by some as the highest expression of human liberty and condemned by others as a veil over deeper structural domination.

— Standard encyclopedic summary

Capitalism is a natural extension of humanity's propensity to exchange. When individuals freely pursue self-interest, an invisible hand guides markets toward the common good.

The division of labor multiplies wealth. The state should enforce contracts and protect property — a limited but necessary role. Beyond that, intervention distorts the price signals that coordinate free exchange.

Capitalism is a mode of production defined by exploitation of wage labor. Market freedom masks asymmetry: the capitalist extracts surplus value, paying workers less than they produce.

This isn't a flaw — it's the mechanism. The state is an instrument of class domination, enforcing bourgeois property relations. Accumulation concentrates wealth until its contradictions become unsustainable.

AI-Assisted Comparison
All three authors describe capitalism — but they disagree on its fundamental nature. Smith sees the state as a limited partner enforcing contracts. Marx sees the same state as a tool of class oppression. Rothbard goes furthest: the state isn't just unnecessary — it's the enemy.

On the source of wealth: Smith credits the invisible hand and division of labor. Marx identifies exploitation of surplus value. Rothbard attributes all dysfunction to government interference.
