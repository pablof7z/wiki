The obvious question
"What stops Wikifreedia from becoming the next Wikipedia?"

Not our good intentions. Not a promise. The architecture.

Entries on Wikifreedia don't live on our servers. They live on an open protocol called Nostr — the same protocol used by millions of people to publish content that no company or government can take down.

That means: we can't edit your entry. We can't delete it. We can't lock the page. We can't revert your work because an admin disagrees with you. Even if we wanted to — and someday, someone at Wikifreedia might want to — the system won't let them.

Wikipedia's corruption isn't a people problem. Good people built it. The problem is that the architecture allows capture. A single database, controlled by a single organization, protected by a single set of admins. Of course it got captured. Any system that can be captured will be.

Wikifreedia is built so that capture is structurally impossible. Not because we're better people — because the protocol doesn't have an admin button.

Wikipedia's problem was never the editors. It was that the architecture gave editors the power to become gatekeepers. We removed the gate.
The hard question
"Without gatekeepers, won't it just be chaos?"
No editorial board. No admins. No locks. So what stops Wikifreedia from drowning in spam, conspiracy theories, and hate speech?

Wikipedia's answer: appoint an editorial priesthood to decide what's true. The result is a political body with political incentives pretending to be neutral.

Our answer: Web of Trust. Instead of an editorial board deciding whose voice counts, you decide — by choosing who you trust. Your trust network filters the noise. The more people in your chain vouch for an author, the more visible that author's work becomes to you.

No one is silenced. No one is banned. But spam doesn't propagate — because no one trusts spammers.

Isolated nodes = invisible. No trust = no reach.

Wikipedia flattens every topic into a single line — one version wins, the rest are erased. That's not knowledge. That's a vote disguised as truth.

Wikifreedia captures the full topology of a subject: clusters of powerful, conflicting perspectives. Each cluster is internally coherent and externally contested. You don't get a single answer — you get a map of the disagreement, with every faction visible, every argument traceable.

Flat truth is propaganda. Three-dimensional truth is knowledge you can actually navigate.

Why we built this

"We wanted to give a voice to the voiceless, but what emerged was one of the most effective organs of Establishment propaganda in history."

That's not us talking. That's Larry Sanger — the man who co-founded Wikipedia.

Wikipedia has 833 administrators, 16 bureaucrats, and 15 arbitration committee members. Of the 62 most powerful accounts, 85% are anonymous. No one knows who these people are, who pays them, or what agendas they serve.

Sanger suspects many of them are paid operatives. WikiScanner found edits coming from CIA headquarters in Langley — removing Iraq War casualty counts, deleting satellite images of Guantanamo Bay. The FBI edited articles too. As Sanger puts it: "Intelligence agencies use Wikipedia as a one-stop shop to make people believe things."

"Wikipedia is completely dishonest and completely controlled on questions that matter. The word for it is propaganda." — Larry Sanger, co-founder of Wikipedia, Tucker Carlson Show, 2025

Since 2017, Wikipedia has maintained a source blacklist that bans Fox News, the New York Post, the Daily Caller, the Epoch Times, and The Federalist. If your defenders only exist in blacklisted sources, you will not be defended. The blacklist was created right after Trump's election. Coincidence.

The COVID lab leak article asserted, in Wikipedia's own voice, that "there is no evidence supporting laboratory involvement" — a claim now disputed by the FBI and the Department of Energy. On the talk page, prior "consensus" was brandished to reject including updated intelligence findings. The Gaza genocide article asserts Israel committed genocide, with almost no coverage of dissenting views — including those of both Wikipedia founders.

This is not a broken process. This is the process working exactly as designed. Wikipedia's own neutrality policy says "articles must not take sides" — and then its consensus mechanism does nothing but pick sides, call the winner "neutral," and lock the page.

We didn't build Wikifreedia because we think we're smarter than Wikipedia's editors. We built it because no one should have that power. Not them. Not us. Not anyone.

"The power to declare what is known is nearly the power to rule the world. No small group — no person, corporation, oligarchy, or cadre of insiders — should wield such power." — Larry Sanger, Encyclosphere address, 2019

Knowledge isn't a single document that gets voted on. It's a living argument between people who see the world differently. The moment you flatten that into one "neutral" page, you haven't created knowledge — you've created propaganda with citations.

Wikifreedia exists because the disagreement is the knowledge.
