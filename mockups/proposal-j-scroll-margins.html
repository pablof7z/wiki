Capitalism

Capitalism is an economic system characterized by private ownership of the means of production, in which investment decisions are made by private actors and prices, production, and distribution of goods are determined by competition in markets.

The system emerged in early modern Europe and expanded through industrialization into the dominant global economic model. Its proponents argue it generates prosperity through competition and innovation.

Critics contend the system produces inequality, exploitation, and periodic crises that threaten social stability. The debate over capitalism's merits has shaped political discourse for over two centuries.

The relationship between capitalism and the state remains one of the most contested questions in political economy. Some view the state as essential to capitalism's functioning; others see it as fundamentally opposed.

The apparent freedom of market exchange is celebrated by some as the highest expression of human liberty and questioned by others as a veil over deeper structural forces.

Contemporary debate centers on whether capitalism can be reformed to address its shortcomings, or whether its fundamental structure makes such reform impossible.
