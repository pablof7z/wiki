Encyclopedia Entry
Ivermectin

Ivermectin is an antiparasitic drug discovered in the 1970s, awarded the Nobel Prize in 2015 for its role in treating river blindness and lymphatic filariasis. During the COVID-19 pandemic, it became the subject of intense debate over its potential antiviral properties.

Some physicians reported positive clinical outcomes using ivermectin as an early treatment for COVID-19, while public health authorities cautioned that large-scale randomized controlled trials had not demonstrated clear efficacy. Regulatory agencies issued warnings against its use outside approved indications.

The controversy extended beyond pharmacology into questions about institutional trust, media coverage, and the relationship between regulatory bodies and pharmaceutical companies.

The ivermectin question isn't really about ivermectin — it's about whether the regulatory framework is structurally capable of evaluating a cheap generic during an emergency where billions of dollars are at stake.

The Emergency Use Authorization legally required that "no adequate, approved, and available alternatives" exist. This created a structural incentive to dismiss any potential early treatment — the entire vaccine program depended on there being no alternative.

Both "it's a miracle drug" and "it's horse paste" are wrong. The real scandal is that we built a system incapable of honestly evaluating the question.
