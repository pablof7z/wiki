Encyclopedia Entry
Capitalism

Capitalism is an economic system characterized by private ownership of the means of production and the operation of markets for the allocation of goods and services. Under capitalism, investment decisions are made by private actors, and prices are determined by competition in free markets.

The system has been the subject of extensive analysis and critique since its emergence. Its proponents argue that it generates prosperity through competition and innovation, while its critics contend that it produces inequality, exploitation, and periodic crises. The role of the state within capitalism remains one of the most contested questions in political economy.

Capitalism is a natural extension of humanity's propensity to truck, barter, and exchange. When individuals freely pursue self-interest, an invisible hand guides markets toward socially beneficial outcomes.

The division of labor multiplies wealth beyond what planning could achieve. The state's role is limited but necessary: enforce contracts, protect property, provide public goods. Beyond that, intervention distorts price signals.

Capitalism is a mode of production defined by private ownership and exploitation of wage labor. Market freedom masks a fundamental asymmetry: the capitalist extracts surplus value, paying workers less than they produce.

This isn't a flaw — it's the mechanism. The state is an instrument of class domination, enforcing bourgeois property relations. Accumulation concentrates wealth until contradictions become unsustainable.
