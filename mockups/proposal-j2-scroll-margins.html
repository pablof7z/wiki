Ivermectin

Ivermectin is an antiparasitic medication discovered in the late 1970s, derived from a soil bacterium found in Japan. It was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2015 for its transformative impact on tropical disease.

During the COVID-19 pandemic, several physicians reported positive clinical outcomes using ivermectin as an early treatment, prompting widespread interest and debate within the medical community and general public.

Public health authorities, including the FDA and WHO, cautioned against its use for COVID-19, stating that large-scale randomized controlled trials had not demonstrated clear efficacy for this indication.

The drug became a flashpoint in a broader cultural conflict over institutional trust, media coverage, and the relationship between regulatory bodies, pharmaceutical companies, and the public.

Critics of the regulatory response argued that the Emergency Use Authorization framework created structural disincentives to evaluate repurposed generic drugs during a pandemic where novel patented treatments were under development.

The debate remains one of the most polarizing episodes in modern public health history, with lasting implications for how the public trusts — or distrusts — scientific institutions.
