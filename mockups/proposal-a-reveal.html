Capitalism
Capitalism is a natural extension of humanity's innate propensity to truck, barter, and exchange. When individuals are free to pursue their own economic self-interest, an invisible hand guides the market toward outcomes that benefit society as a whole. The division of labor, enabled by free exchange, multiplies productive capacity beyond what any central planner could achieve.

The role of the state is limited: enforce contracts, protect property rights, and provide public goods that no private actor would supply. Beyond this, government intervention distorts the natural price signals that coordinate millions of independent decisions.

Capitalism
Capitalism is a historically specific mode of production defined by the private ownership of the means of production and the exploitation of wage labor. The apparent freedom of the market masks a fundamental asymmetry: the capitalist extracts surplus value from workers, paying them less than the value they produce. This is not a flaw in the system — it is the system.

The state under capitalism is not a neutral arbiter but an instrument of class domination, enforcing the property relations that sustain bourgeois power. Capital accumulation inevitably concentrates wealth and deepens the contradictions that will lead to capitalism's own dissolution.

Capitalism
Capitalism, in its pure form, is a system of voluntary exchange between free individuals, rooted in the absolute right to self-ownership and private property. Every government intervention — taxation, regulation, licensing — is a coercive violation of individual rights that distorts market outcomes and creates the very problems it claims to solve.

The state itself is the greatest threat to capitalism, not its guardian. A truly free market requires no state at all — private arbitration, insurance, and defense agencies can provide every service government monopolizes, more efficiently and without coercion.
